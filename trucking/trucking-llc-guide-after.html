How to Start a Trucking Company & Register an LLC

The order you file things in matters more than most new carriers realize. Here is the exact sequence to launch a legal for-hire trucking company in the United States — with current costs and timelines.

By J. Rivera, Freight Compliance Writer Updated July 16, 2026 Reviewed for accuracy
Short answer

To start a trucking company, form an LLC, get an EIN, then register with the FMCSA for a USDOT number (free) and MC operating authority ($300). Before your authority activates, file a BOC-3 process agent and post insurance — a minimum of $750,000 in primary liability. Most for-hire carriers reach active authority in about three to five weeks.

USDOT number vs. MC number: what's the difference?

A USDOT number is your safety ID. An MC number is your permission to haul for hire. Interstate for-hire carriers usually need both.

This is the single most common point of confusion for new carriers, so it is worth getting straight before you file anything. The two credentials do different jobs, and mixing them up is a frequent cause of stalled applications.

USDOT Number

Safety identifier

Issued by the FMCSA to track your safety record — inspections, audits, and crash data. It appears on your truck door and your carrier snapshot.

Cost: Free. Renewed every two years or whenever company details change.

MC Authority

Operating authority

Your legal permission to transport regulated freight for hire across state lines. Brokers and shippers check that it is active before they book you.

Cost: $300 per authority type, plus a mandatory review period before it activates.

Interstate vs. intrastateYou need MC operating authority when you cross state lines hauling regulated freight. If you operate entirely within one state, your state DOT rules apply instead — check your home state's requirements.

→The launch sequence, in the right order

File these out of order and you can end up re-registering your USDOT number, updating MC records, and reissuing insurance certificates under a new name — weeks of avoidable delay. This is the sequence that keeps everything clean.

1

Form your LLC

Register a limited liability company with your state's Secretary of State. Trucking carries heavy liability exposure — one accident or cargo claim can reach six or seven figures — so the entity shields your personal assets. Reserve your business name and appoint a registered agent.

Entity first
2

Get an EIN from the IRS

Apply online for a free Employer Identification Number. Every FMCSA application asks for your legal entity name and EIN, so you need this in hand before you open the federal portal.

Free · ~10 min
3

Open a business bank account

Take your EIN and Articles of Organization to any bank. Running every dollar through a dedicated account keeps your liability protection intact and makes tax season and factoring far simpler.

Keeps the shield intact
4

Register for your USDOT number

Register through the FMCSA's online system — now running on the newer MOTUS platform, which requires a Login.gov account. You'll classify your operation, cargo, and power units. The USDOT number is typically issued shortly after a clean submission.

Free · Safety ID
5

Apply for MC operating authority

File for Motor Carrier authority to haul regulated freight for hire. The fee is $300 per authority type. You can request it in the same session as your USDOT number.

$300 · Operating authority
6

File your BOC-3 process agent

A BOC-3 designates a legal representative to receive service of process in every state you operate in. Your authority will not activate without it. A service provider files it electronically — make sure the name and address exactly match your FMCSA record, or activation stalls.

Required to activate
7

Buy insurance and have it filed

Purchase primary liability coverage — a $750,000 federal minimum for general freight, though most brokers require $1 million. Your insurer posts proof electronically to the FMCSA. Authority stays pending until that filing lands.

$750K minimum
8

Set up ongoing compliance

Once active, register for IRP apportioned plates and IFTA through your home state, file Form 2290 (Heavy Vehicle Use Tax) if your truck is 55,000 lbs or more, pay your UCR, and enroll in a drug and alcohol testing consortium tied to the FMCSA Clearinghouse.

Keeps you legal

Choosing your operation and cargo type

Your cargo type drives your insurance cost, licensing, and rates — pick it before you price coverage.

New carriers most often start in one of these lanes. Dry van is the simplest entry point; specialized freight pays more but carries higher cost and complexity.

  • Dry van — general enclosed freight, the most common way to start.
  • Reefer — refrigerated freight; higher rates, more maintenance.
  • Flatbed / step deck — construction and industrial loads.
  • Hotshot — heavy-duty pickup and trailer; lower startup cost.
  • Tanker — liquids or gases; requires special endorsements.
  • Car hauler — vehicle transport; higher liability.

What it costs to start in 2026

Frequently asked questions

Do I need an LLC to start a trucking company?
No. The FMCSA will issue a USDOT number and MC authority to a sole proprietor under your personal name. Most owner-operators still form an LLC first because trucking carries large liability exposure — and forming the entity afterward forces you to re-file your USDOT, MC, and insurance records under the new name.
What is the difference between a USDOT number and an MC number?
A USDOT number is your federal safety identifier, used by the FMCSA to track inspections, audits, and crash data. An MC number is your operating authority — the legal permission to haul regulated freight for hire across state lines. Many interstate for-hire carriers need both.
How much does it cost to start a trucking company in 2026?
Government fees are modest: the USDOT number is free and MC authority is $300 per authority type. State LLC filing runs roughly $50 to $500, and a BOC-3 is about $30 to $100. Insurance is the real cost — commonly $1,200 to $3,000+ per month for a new authority in its first two years.
How long does it take to get MC authority?
A USDOT number is often issued shortly after a complete online submission. MC operating authority takes longer because of a mandatory review period, and it stays pending until your BOC-3 and insurance are on file. Most new carriers reach active authority in about three to five weeks.
How much insurance does a new trucking company need?
Federal law sets a minimum of $750,000 in primary liability for general freight, though most brokers require $1,000,000. Hazardous materials require $5,000,000. Carriers also typically carry about $100,000 in cargo insurance, plus physical damage coverage if the truck is financed.
Do I need a CDL to own a trucking company?
Only if you plan to drive a commercial vehicle yourself. If you operate as a non-driving owner and hire drivers, you do not need a personal CDL — but every driver you employ must hold the correct class of commercial license.
